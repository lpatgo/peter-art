Diversity Statement

Diversity begins with one's own self-awareness of positionality. I enter into the classroom with an understanding of how my age, race, gender, class, and ability factor into course design and teaching power dynamics. I believe in pedagogical methods that recognize the intersectional, fluid nature of identity and dismantle hierarchical structures within the classroom itself by establishing valued space for students' ideas. In other words, one must foster inclusivity by promoting the individual's voice.

It is my job as a professor to guide students through their own self-discovery as artists and people. This means facilitating activities that generate thoughtful discussion, which should equally incite deep listening. Students will begin to take other perspectives into consideration and value these varying worldviews. For example, I often encourage students to display their work coupled with storytelling. Here, they might share a childhood experience, reflect upon their parents' influential political or religious beliefs, or even open-up about post-graduation anxieties. As a large group and one-on-one, I also pose open-ended questions to the students, asking them to reflect upon their lived experiences and how these may relate to or shape different factors of their identities.

While students consider their own backgrounds, I am sure to equally contextualize the aesthetic, political, and historic nature of the artworks we study in class. My assignments, while employing traditional mediums, address the cultural factors at play, and students can begin assessing what constitutes the "canon," who makes these decisions, and who might be left out. I, myself, contemplate these same questions through course design and include artistic voices often missing from traditional western art history.

A clear example of this is in my figure painting assignment. First, we discuss the role of the female nude in art history, and how the male gaze has functioned as a defining factor in this phenomenon. We establish respect and appropriate etiquette towards those modeling as well. The next class, students create acrylic studies of a nude model, who is asked to make poses that represent different emotional states. Following this, we consider our feelings about representing the body and think through ways to articulate more autonomy for the figure on canvas. In the past, one class decided to increase the scale by painting on large found cardboard and intensifying the contrast with a monochromatic palette. Students manipulated scale and color to empower the nude figure, and they participated in meaningful conversations on how these types of artistic decisions relate back to representation.

A diverse classroom itself is only the first step towards creating an inclusive experience. As a professor of art, I design my curriculum and assignments to cultivate the students' individual voices and guide them towards their own sense of self-awareness.
